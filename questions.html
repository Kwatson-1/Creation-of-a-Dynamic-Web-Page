Analysis: places emphasis on investigating a problem and its requirements for the purpose of gathering information to inform a conclusion or recommend a specific course of action.

Design: places emphasis on the conceptualization of a solution to meet requirements and often does not concern itself with implementation.

Website architectural requirements: are often set by a user and/or business for the purpose of defining the technical, functional, and visual component of a website prior to its design, development, and deployment.

Hierarchy: is a design structure that follows a simple top-down to website design and typically begins with a home page followed by the top-level pages, and then subsequent subpages or child pages.

Navigation: design structure describes how different pages on a site are organized and connected to one another. Good navigation design promotes usability and improves the overall user experience.

User-interface design requirements: outlines the expectations for creating a user-interface. When designing a website these requirements aim to ensure that a websiteâ€™s UI meets expectations to subsequently improve the overall experience of users.

Programming controls: dictate how a program/application makes decisions or organizes its activities. An example of a program control might be a method that is performed following execution of a prior function, specified conditions being met or user input.

Design Structures: determines how information is organized and interconnected on a website. Three essential web structures includes sequences (linear navigation), hierarchies (top down navigation) and webs (associative navigation).

Website testing procedures: are steps to ensure that an application functions properly and runs smooths and securely prior to its release, this includes:

  • 1. Functionality testing: ensuring the website, database connections, links, cookies, and forms for submitting/retrieving information all work.
  • 2. Usability testing: combines functionality testing with assessment of the overall user experience primarily concerning navigation and content.
  • 3. Interface testing: for checking proper connectivity between any components like APIs and web services as well as testing overall fault tolerance.
  • 4. Compatibility testing: ensuring the website functions and displays correctly in different browsers and on different devices.
  • 5. Performance testing: testing the website with different internet speeds and with normal and peak traffic.

    Website debugging methods: procedures used to diagnose defects or problems that interrupt or prevent proper operation within a website, these include:

  • Brute force method: examining of all relevant memory stacks and observing of any potential places of inconsistency with expected data.
  • Back tracking method: beginning from the source of a detected symptom and following functions back to their callers until detecting the issue.
  • Cause elimination method: utilizing deduction and induction to reach a conclusion based on evidence and reason.
  • External tools: utilization of third-party debugging/developer tools.
  • Unit testing: documentation/testing of proper website functionality and expected outcomes as part of a cause elimination method.

  • Responsive web design strategy: creation of websites that function optimally regardless of the device or website it is used on.
  • Consistency maintenance: beginning from the source of a detected symptom and following functions back to their callers until detecting the issue.
  • Responsive images: utilizing deduction and induction to reach a conclusion based on evidence and reason.
  • Efficient navigation system: utilization of third-party debugging/developer tools.
  • Web accessibility: documentation/testing of proper website functionality and expected outcomes as part of a cause elimination method.
